Exercise 1: Explain it like I am 5 (ELI5)
Application: Quickly understand complex topics or prepare simple explanations.
"Explain [TOPIC] to me like I am 5 (ELI5)"

You can either type a topic or upload a pdf of a research paper (click the + plus button then click upload). Replace [TOPIC] with: "cloud computing", "DNA", "quantum physics", "offside rule in football" etc.

Exercise 2: Devil's Advocate (Blind Spots)
Application: Stress-test your challenges, new ideas, or decisions before finalising them.
"Act as a constructive devil's advocate. First identify potential weaknesses or blind spots, then suggest alternative perspectives. Be specific and challenging but constructive. My scenario is: [scenario here]"

Add in your own scenario: "I want to move from sales to leadership role", "dealing with a difficult client", provide some useful context as well. You could upload a document instead of typing a scenario e.g. upload your new travel policy

Exercise 3: Second Pair of Eyes (Critical Peer Review)
Application: Interrogate a paper, report, or draft to identify overreach, hidden assumptions, and missing context before submission or circulation.
"Act as a critical but fair peer reviewer. Answer the following questions clearly and directly. Question one: What can and cannot be legitimately concluded from the methodology and results alone? Where does the abstract overreach? Question two: What did this study not measure that would have changed what it found? What is the methodology quietly assuming it never defends? Question three: What debate is this paper entering? Who is it responding to, and what could those people respond with? Question four: What is the most important paper missing from the references? Here is the paper or extract: [paste text or upload document]"

Paste an abstract, methods section, results, or full draft. Upload a PDF or Word file if easier. Use before submission, response to reviewers, or internal review.

💡 You could experiment with alternative versions of the peer‑review prompt.
  • Hostile Reviewer: "Act as a sceptical reviewer at a top‑tier journal. Assume the burden of proof has not been met. Identify only weaknesses, omissions, and risks of misinterpretation."
Exercise 4: Breaking Down Large Tasks (Risk Assessment)
Application: Break a large or uncertain project into stakeholders, milestones, risks, and mitigations before committing time or resources.
"I'm planning a project to [one sentence description]. Who are the main stakeholders and what are the key milestones?"
"Based on that project, what are the five most likely risks? For each, rate the likelihood (high/medium/low) and potential impact (high/medium/low)."
"For the three highest-priority risks, suggest one practical mitigation for each. Keep them realistic for a small team with limited budget."
"Now combine all of this into a one-page risk assessment summary I could share with my manager. Use plain language and include a simple table for the risks."

Start with Prompt 1 and reuse the outputs step‑by‑step. You can paste text between prompts or upload a short project brief instead.

💡 This works well for projects that feel overwhelming or politically sensitive.
Responsible AI (Best Practices)
The Human in the Loop: AI is a tool to enhance your work, not replace your judgment. Always keep these three pillars in mind.
🤝
Human-Centric Approach
  • It's your assistant (augment over replace).
  • Protect critical skills: use AI to enhance them.
  • Target repetitive manual tasks first.
✅
Practical Checks
  • Always double-check the output.
  • Be Transparent: "I used AI for this."
  • Protect your data (use IT approved AI tools).
⚖️
Responsibility & Ethics
  • Watch out for AI's blind spots (bias/hallucinations).
  • Own the results: AI doesn't take the blame.
  • Align your usage of AI with your values.
It's Your Teammate (The Conversation)
The Reality: You rarely get a perfect draft from a human colleague on the first try—don't expect it from AI. The magic happens in the follow-up.
🚀 Use the "Teammate" Boilerplate Prompt

As illustrated on the right side above, stop asking for instant, giant deliverables. Instead, frame the interaction as a guided collaboration using this structure:

"I want you to help me [INSERT BIG TASK HERE], but don't try to solve it all at once. Instead, act as a helpful guide..."
🤝
The "Teammate" Mindset

Stop treating Copilot like a vending machine (input coin -> get product). Treat it like a clever intern.

If the output isn't right, don't rewrite the prompt from scratch. Just talk to it. Tell it what it did wrong and guide it to the answer.

Try these follow-up phrases:
"Actually, can you make it more formal?"
"That's good, but what if we tried a friendlier approach?"
"Can you expand on that second point? It's a bit too brief."
Practical Prompting (The 1-2-3 Method)
The Golden Rule: Better inputs equal better outputs. Follow this 3-step flow for every prompt to get the best results.
1.
Give Context

Provide relevant background information (Role, Data, Reports).

2.
Make Request

Be specific about exactly what you want the AI to do.

"Review my email for clarity..."
3.
Explain Purpose

Share why you need this (The "So That").

"...to maximize the chances of a quick response."
